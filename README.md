## What a USDT Debit Card Actually Is

A USDT debit card is a payment card (Visa or Mastercard) that draws directly from a USDT balance instead of a bank account. When you make a purchase, the issuer automatically converts the required amount of USDT to the merchant's fiat currency at the live exchange rate. The conversion happens in the background; you never see it. To the merchant, the card behaves like any other Visa or Mastercard.

Most USDT debit cards fall into one of two custody models. **Custodial cards** — the majority of the market — keep your USDT on the issuer's balance sheet and authorise spend against it; account recovery works like a bank, with KYC, password reset, and support tickets. **Self-custody cards** (Gnosis Pay, Kast, MetaMask) attach the card to a smart-contract wallet you control, so each spend shows up as an onchain authorisation. The trade-off: faster setup on the custodial side, full key control on the self-custody side.

Within those two buckets, cards vary on three axes that actually hit your statement: **top-up fee** (charged when you deposit USDT onto the card), **spending fee** (charged per transaction), and **FX fee** (charged when the transaction currency differs from the card's base currency). A card that advertises "no monthly fee" can still cost you 2–3% per purchase if those three fees stack.
